Chapter 2 Market Diagnosis

2.1 Purpose of Market Diagnosis

The single most common failure mode in pack strategy is deploying the wrong solution to the right problem, or a correct solution to the wrong problem. Market Diagnosis is the structured analytical process that prevents both errors. It produces a definitive answer to four questions before any pack design work begins:

  • What type of problem does this market have — brand problem, price problem, pack problem, or execution problem?
  • Is there genuine incremental volume to capture, or would a new pack primarily cannibalize existing portfolio?
  • Where is the opportunity — which consumer segment, which channel, which occasion?
  • Is a pack intervention the right response, or is another RGM lever more appropriate?

Skipping or compressing this step has predictable consequences: over-estimated volume projections, under-estimated cannibalization, wrong channel prioritization, and pack designs that fail to solve the underlying consumer barrier.

2.2 Five-Step Diagnosis Process

Step 1: Affordability Context

Establish the macro affordability baseline before conducting any category or brand analysis. This step calibrates what "affordable" means in this market, for this consumer segment, at this point in time.

Input

Source

What It Tells You

Daily Disposable Income (DDI) by consumer segment

Nielsen Household Panel; government statistical data; OU Finance

Absolute affordability ceiling. Benchmark: entry pack should represent 3–6% of DDI in most emerging markets.

Inflation trend (food & beverage, 12–24 months)

Central bank / statistical bureau data

Rate at which purchasing power is being eroded. Rapid inflation compresses DDI faster than price adjustments can track.

Income segment distribution by geography

Household panel; census data

Identifies where affordability-constrained consumers concentrate — critical for channel prioritization.

Historical pack-price index

Internal pricing data vs. CPI trend

Shows whether pack prices have risen faster or slower than consumer income. Gap = affordability stress signal.

Affordability Index = (Current Pack Price ÷ Local Magic Price Point) × 100. An index above 110 signals meaningful affordability stress requiring intervention. Below 90 signals headroom to premiumize rather than discount.

Step 2: Category and Brand Participation Analysis

Establish whether the market has a participation gap (consumers who could buy but do not) and whether that gap is driven by price, brand, or product availability.

Metric

Definition

Diagnostic Signal

Category Penetration

% of households that purchased any NARTD SSD in the past 3 months

Below 40% in most emerging markets = significant headroom; above 70% = penetration-mature, focus on frequency and premiumization

Brand Penetration

% of households that purchased the specific brand in the past 3 months

Gap between category pen and brand pen = competitive loss or occasion-specific underperformance

Entry-Point Participation

% of category buyers who purchased a pack below the EPP threshold

Low entry-point share = price barrier is blocking recruitment; high entry-point share with flat penetration = brand or equity barrier

Purchase Frequency

Average purchases per buyer per year

Low frequency among existing light buyers = occasion gap; address with frequency pack, not entry pack

Lapsed Buyer Rate

% of prior buyers who did not purchase in the past 6 months

High lapsed rate + affordability stress = affordability pack priority; high lapsed rate without affordability stress = equity or execution issue

Step 5: Problem Classification and Go / No-Go Decision

The diagnostic must conclude with a clear problem classification before any pack solution is considered. The most common error is solving a brand problem with a pack solution, or an execution problem with a pricing change.

Diagnostic Indicators

Correct Response

Pack Response?

Brand Problem

Penetration flat despite competitive price; brand equity scores declining; lapsed buyer rate rising independently of price

Marketing and brand activation; consumer occasion communication; not a pack solution

No — pack will not solve a brand equity deficit

Price Problem

Penetration declining as prices rose above affordability threshold; transaction loss correlated with price index movement; consumer research confirms price barrier

Entry pack at EPP and/or Magic Price Point; affordability pack resizing

Yes — this is the primary use case

Pack Problem

Consumer willing to buy but citing wrong format, wrong size, or wrong occasion fit; competitive packs at same price outperforming

Pack format redesign; occasion-aligned size innovation

Yes — but design must start from consumer occasion, not price alone

Execution Problem

Distribution gaps in target channels; poor cold availability; POSM absent; outlet compliance below standard

Execution standards enforcement; RED compliance; distribution drive

No — a new pack will fail for the same execution reasons as the existing pack

2.5 Key Assumptions and Risk Flags

Key Assumption: The diagnosis framework in this chapter assumes that consumer research data (penetration, frequency, occasion, price sensitivity) is available at minimum at Nielsen/Kantar panel level. Where panel data is unavailable — as is common in Cambodia, parts of Vietnam GT, and smaller markets — proxy approaches using distributor sell-out data, outlet audit results, and management estimates are acceptable for initial hypothesis generation but must be flagged as lower-confidence inputs in the business case.

[FLAG: additional input required — Vietnam General Trade: As of early 2026, approximately 66% of promotional spend in VN is untracked at the off-invoice level (Actual=0 for all off-invoice line items in the SAP system). This significantly limits the ability to model cannibalization accurately in GT channels. Until SAP CCS is mandated for VN off-invoice tracking, Vietnam-specific cannibalization projections should carry a wide confidence interval (±20–30%).]
